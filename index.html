The number e — nature’s favourite growth

Compound interest, calculus, and a bridge to complex numbers — why 2.71828… feels almost magical.

In school we meet π first. Then, quietly, another constant appears: e ≈ 2.718281828459045… It is the heartbeat of smooth growth, of logarithms, and of how rates of change mirror the thing being measured. Once you see it, it keeps turning up — population models, cooling coffee, probability, and the spiral of compound interest.

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783)

Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler (often spelled “Leonard” in English, but his name is Leonhard) was one of the most prolific minds in the history of mathematics. He shaped notation we still use, connected exponentials with trigonometry through complex numbers, and studied the constant that now bears his name. His work helped turn e from a computational curiosity into a central pillar of analysis.

From (1 + 1/n)n to e

A famous definition is a limit as the integer n grows (often described as taking many tiny steps — so the step size 1/n is small):

e = limn→∞ (1 + 1/n)n.

It is easy to misread this as “n very small”; what matters is many compounding periods (large n), so each period adds a tiny proportional slice. As n increases, the value climbs and settles toward e.

£1 at 100% interest — compounded “all the time”

Imagine £1 invested at 100% annual interest. If interest is paid once at year’s end, you have £2. If it is paid twice (50% each half-year), you end with (1 + ½)² = £2.25. Twelve times a month-style compounding: (1 + 1/12)12 ≈ £2.613.

Let n be the number of equal periods in the year, each at rate 1/n. The balance is (1 + 1/n)n. As n → ∞ — compounding in finer and finer increments — the amount approaches £e ≈ £2.71828…, not infinity. Continuous compounding is the mathematical idealisation of that limit.

Irrational and transcendental

Irrational: e cannot be written as a fraction p/q with integers p and q. Its decimal expansion never settles into a repeating block.

Transcendental: A polynomial in x is a finite sum of whole-number powers of x with fixed coefficients — for example 5x³ − 2x + 7 or simply x² − 2. (There is no division by x, no infinite series, and no exponentials inside — just powers.) A number is a root of a polynomial if substituting it for x makes the expression zero. The number e is transcendental: it is not a root of any non-zero polynomial whose coefficients are all integers. By contrast, √2 is irrational but still satisfies x² − 2 = 0, so it is algebraic, not transcendental. (Charles Hermite proved the transcendence of e in 1873; Ferdinand von Lindemann later proved π transcendental — which ties into eiπ = −1.)

Exponentials and derivatives — the 2x, 3x, ex picture

Plot an exponential and its derivative on the same axes: the two curves are unmistakably the same shape — one is just a vertical stretch of the other. For 2x, the derivative is a gentle squeeze downward: it hugs below the original graph. For 3x, the derivative is a gentle pull upward: it rides above. So base 2 grows a bit “too slowly” relative to its slope, and base 3 a bit “too fast”. That invites a natural question: is there a Goldilocks base — a number a for which ax and its derivative are exactly the same curve? There is, and only one: e. How each derivative is calculated (limit definition first)

We start from the definition of the derivative — the limit of the difference quotient — then specialise to 2x, 3x, and ex. The number limh→0 (ah − 1) / h turns out to depend only on the base a; that number is what we call ln a (natural logarithm of a).

1. Any positive base: f(x) = ax (a > 0, a ≠ 1)

f′(x) = limh→0 (f(x + h) − f(x)) / h = limh→0 (ax+h − ax) / h.

Factor out ax (it does not depend on h):  = limh→0 ax (ah − 1) / h = ax · limh→0 (ah − 1) / h.

The second factor is a constant that depends only on a. Write L(a) = limh→0 (ah − 1) / h.

So  d/dx (ax) = L(a) · ax. In calculus we name this constant L(a) = ln a.

Try tiny h on a calculator: (2h − 1) / h creeps toward ≈ 0.693, (3h − 1) / h toward ≈ 1.099. Those values are ln 2 and ln 3.

2. Derivative of 2x

Put a = 2 in the formula:  d/dx (2x) = 2x · limh→0 (2h − 1) / h.

That limit is ln 2 ≈ 0.693, which is less than 1.

Hence d/dx (2x) = (ln 2) · 2x — the graph of the derivative is 2x scaled down vertically, so it sits below 2x on the same axes.

3. Derivative of 3x

d/dx (3x) = 3x · limh→0 (3h − 1) / h = (ln 3) · 3x.

Here ln 3 ≈ 1.099 is greater than 1, so the derivative is a vertical stretch of 3x — it lies above the original curve.

4. Derivative of ex — the special base

The number e is chosen (equivalently to your compound-interest limit) so that limh→0 (eh − 1) / h = 1. In other words, L(e) = 1, i.e. ln e = 1.

Then d/dx (ex) = ex · limh→0 (eh − 1) / h = ex · 1 = ex.

So ex is the unique exponential whose derivative is the same function — no extra factor; the curve and its derivative coincide.

Addendum: the same rules via logarithmic differentiation

Let y = ax. Then ln y = x ln a. Differentiate: (1/y) dy/dx = ln a, so dy/dx = y ln a = (ln a) · ax.

For a = 2 or 3, substitute. For a = e, ln e = 1 gives dy/dx = ex immediately.

This is shorter once you trust d/dx (ln x) = 1/x and the chain rule; the limit definition above shows where the factor ln a comes from.

Complex numbers: eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ

Euler’s formula identifies a point on the unit circle with a complex exponential. For real θ,

eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ.

So z = eiπ = cos π + i sin π = −1 — the famous eiπ + 1 = 0 linking e, i, π, 1 and 0. More generally, R eiθ has real part R cos θ and imaginary part R sin θ (your z = R cos π + i R sin π = −R).

Why natural logs use base e

The logarithm ln(x) is the inverse of ex. Because d/dx (ex) = ex, the slope of ln(x) at x is beautifully simple: d/dx ln(x) = 1/x. Integrals like ∫(1/x)dx = ln|x| + C emerge naturally. Change of base is always possible (loga x = ln x / ln a), but base e is the one that aligns growth rates, slopes, and integrals with the least extra clutter — hence “natural”.

A few more appearances

  • Exponential decay: N(t) = N₀ e−kt for radioactive decay and many relaxation processes.
  • Normal distribution: the bell curve’s formula contains e−x²; probabilities integrate to 1 in a way that leans on e.
  • Compound growth: any process whose instantaneous rate is proportional to the amount present leads to e.
